Turn-taking is a fundamental aspect of spoken dialogue. Human interlocutors coordinate who speaks and when with remarkable precision, producing gaps and overlaps that are often shorter than 200 ms [1, 2]. Such timing cannot be explained by a simple reactive account, in which a listener waits for silence before starting to speak, because the latencies of speech production alone exceed the observed gap durations [3]. Instead, listeners appear to continuously predict how the current utterance will unfold and prepare their response in advance. Spoken dialogue systems, on the other hand, still rely predominantly on silence thresholds to decide when the user has finished speaking [4]. This strategy results in sluggish responses after long pauses and frequent interruptions during hesitations, and it remains one of the main obstacles to natural conversational interaction [5].

A large body of work has therefore addressed the modeling of turn-taking from observable conversational behavior. Early studies identified prosodic, syntactic, and pragmatic cues that signal turn completion [1, 6], and later work trained classifiers to detect turn shifts at pauses or to predict upcoming backchannels [7, 8, 9]. More recently, continuous models based on recurrent neural networks have been proposed, which predict future speech activity at every frame rather than only at candidate points [10, 11]. Note that these approaches share a common characteristic. They are trained on specific turn-taking events, such as turn shifts, backchannels, or interruptions, which have to be defined and extracted beforehand. In other words, the model designer must decide in advance which phenomena are relevant, where their boundaries lie, and how ambiguous cases should be treated. These definitions vary considerably across studies, which makes results difficult to compare, and the resulting event inventories inevitably discard much of the information present in the raw interaction. Furthermore, the amount of labeled data obtained in this way is limited, since the events of interest are sparse relative to the total duration of the conversation.

Self-supervised learning offers a way around this bottleneck. In speech processing, models trained to predict masked or future portions of the signal have produced representations that transfer well to a wide range of downstream tasks [12, 13], and similar objectives underlie recent progress in language modeling [14]. The voice activity of the two parties in a dyadic conversation is an attractive target for such an objective. It is trivially obtained from the audio, it is available at every point in time, and it implicitly encodes the turn-taking dynamics of the interaction, including turn shifts, backchannels, pauses, and overlaps.

In this paper, we propose Voice Activity Projection (VAP), a general and self-supervised objective for modeling turn-taking in dyadic conversation. The model consumes the raw waveforms of the two speakers and is trained to predict a discrete representation of their joint voice activity over the upcoming two seconds, without any event-specific annotation. Turn-taking events are not used during training but can instead be read off the predicted projections at test time. We train the model on two-party telephone conversations from the Switchboard corpus [15] and evaluate it on the prediction of turn shifts and backchannels, comparing against supervised event-classification baselines and prior continuous models [10, 11]. Experimental results show that the proposed model matches or outperforms the supervised baselines on these events, even though it was never explicitly trained on them. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first model of turn-taking that is trained entirely with a self-supervised objective. We also analyze how performance varies with the amount of training data and examine what the projection representation captures, in order to shed some light on why the objective is effective.
