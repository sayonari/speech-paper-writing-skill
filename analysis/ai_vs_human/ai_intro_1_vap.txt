Voice activity — knowing who is speaking and when — is the most basic observable signal of the coordination that underlies human conversation. Speakers in dialogue take turns with remarkable precision: gaps between turns are typically only a few hundred milliseconds, far shorter than the latency of speech production, which implies that listeners predict upcoming turn completions rather than merely react to them [1, 2]. Reproducing this predictive skill in spoken dialogue systems remains an open problem. Most deployed systems still rely on silence thresholds to decide when the user has finished speaking, which leads to responses that are either sluggish or interruptive, and which ignore conversational phenomena such as backchannels and overlap [3, 4].

Prior computational work on turn-taking has largely framed the problem as supervised classification of specific events. Models have been trained to detect turn shifts at pauses [5], to predict upcoming backchannel opportunities [6], or to identify interruptions and turn holds [7]. While effective for their individual targets, these approaches share two limitations. First, they depend on event labels whose definitions are partly arbitrary: what counts as a turn shift, a backchannel, or a pause depends on annotation choices and threshold parameters, and these choices vary across studies. Second, training a separate model per event type fragments what is arguably a single underlying skill, namely anticipating the near future of the interaction.

In this paper we propose Voice Activity Projection (VAP), a self-supervised objective that sidesteps event definitions altogether. Instead of predicting labeled events, the model continuously predicts the joint voice activity of both speakers in a dyadic conversation over a future window. Because voice activity can be extracted automatically from the audio signal, the approach requires no manual annotation and can be trained on any corpus of two-party spoken dialogue. The future activity window is discretized into a compact codebook of activity patterns, and the model is trained to predict a distribution over these patterns at each time step. Turn-taking events such as turn shifts, holds, and backchannels are then recovered as interpretable projections of this general representation, rather than being built into the training target.

This formulation has several attractive properties. It provides a single model whose output can be queried, in a zero-shot fashion, for a range of turn-taking phenomena. It naturally represents uncertainty about the near future, including situations where both speakers are about to be active. And because the objective is defined on raw voice activity, it transfers across annotation conventions and, potentially, across languages and domains.

We evaluate VAP on two-party telephone conversations, comparing the proposed discrete pattern prediction against independent per-speaker activity regression baselines. We analyze performance on established turn-taking test sets covering shift/hold prediction at pauses, prediction of upcoming backchannels, and discrimination of turn-completion points, and we examine what the model has learned through its behavior at syntactically and prosodically manipulated turn boundaries. Our results show that the projection objective yields a general-purpose turn-taking representation that matches or outperforms task-specific supervised baselines while requiring no labeled data. We release our code and trained models to support future work.
