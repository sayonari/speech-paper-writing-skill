In the past few years, various spoken dialogue systems (SDS) have
been implemented in smartphones, humanoid robots, smart speakers, and the other applications. Despite this rapid growth of the
SDS market, human-robot conversations are still very different from
natural human-human conversations. To increase the naturalness of
dialogue, SDS must imitate a human’s conversational behavior as
much as possible. One human behavior the SDS should imitate is
backchannel (BC). There are two tasks in generating BCs: when the
system should generate BC (BC opportunity) and what kind of BC
form is appropriate (BC category).
In the case of BC opportunity prediction (BOP), numerous studies have investigated various features and methods. The majority of
rule-based methods used pitch as a predictor [1][2]. Other studies
have used machine learning to train prediction models [3][4][5][6].
They evaluated the method with various input features and reported
that the performance with both lexical and acoustic features was signiﬁcantly better than that with only the acoustic features.
On the other hand, few studies have examined the BC category
prediction (BCP). Kawahara et al. [7] classiﬁed BC into four classes:
“un,” “unX2,” “unX3,” and “assessment” using both acoustic and
lexical features. Fujie et al. [8] proposed a method to predict optimal
BC words of SDS using a ﬁnite state transducer (FST) that receives
a user’s utterance and outputs BC words.
In a practical spoken dialogue system, the ASR needs a certain length of processing time to output lexical features from speech

data. However, this lexical feature extraction causes a response delay when it is implemented to actual SDS. This means that, if BC is
predicted on the basis of the lexical features, the timing of backchannel generation of SDS is also delayed. Delayed BC might cause an
unnatural conversation, as BC is a kind of a reﬂex action. Many
researchers have proposed BC prediction methods using the lexical
features. However, most researchers have not discussed the effect
of ASR processing time on obtaining the lexical features. A few researchers have researched BC prediction using lexical features under
one of two assumptions: ASR gives the lexical features with no delay
[5][9] or takes a constant and short 100 ms processing time to output the lexical features [10]. Fujie et al. [8] proposed a method for
constructing an FST that outputs optimal BC words to be uttered by
SDS from a user’s utterance even while the user is speaking. However, they did not show how much the method reduces the delay in
BC word output. In our last study, we proposed a method that can
mitigate the response delay problem in BC prediction [11].
To predict events in the near future, utilization of an early
loss (EL) function has been studied, especially in vision research
[12][13]. However, there is no research that considers utilization
of an attention mechanism with EL. Looking at the success of the
attention mechanism in various ﬁelds [14][15], the mechanism must
contribute to future event prediction using EL. We propose a novel
method to incorporate the attention mechanism with EL functionbased future event prediction.

In this study, we propose a novel BC opportunity and category
prediction method to mitigate the response delay problem caused by
ASR. We incorporate an EL function to train attention-based long
short-term memory (LSTM) models to mitigate the delay problem.
To the best of our knowledge, our paper is the ﬁrst to incorporate an
attention mechanism with EL function-based future event prediction.
