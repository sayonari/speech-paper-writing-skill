When people listen to a conversational partner, they do not remain silent: they produce short response tokens such as English "mm-hm" and "yeah", Japanese "un", or Spanish "sí", commonly called backchannels [1, 2]. These tokens signal continued attention, mark understanding or agreement, and help the speaker decide whether to continue, and they do so without claiming the conversational floor. Backchannels are pervasive across languages, yet they occupy a curious position in linguistic description: because they are short, prosodically reduced, and often excluded from orthographic transcription conventions, they are poorly covered by standard lexical resources, and their inventory and internal structure have rarely been described systematically [3].

Most of what is known about backchannel form comes from language-specific studies based on manual analysis of small datasets [4, 5], or from engineering work that treats backchannels as a single undifferentiated class to be detected or generated by dialogue systems [6, 7]. Both traditions leave basic questions unanswered. What is the inventory of backchannel forms in a given language? Do languages share a common core of forms, for example nasal continuers of the "mm" type, alongside language-specific tokens? And how is variation organized within a language: are different lexical forms, and different prosodic realizations of the same form, associated with different interactional functions? Cross-linguistic answers require a method that does not presuppose a transcription scheme or a predefined category set, since these differ across corpora and languages and would bias any comparison.

In this paper we take a bottom-up approach to these questions. Starting from conversational corpora in a diverse set of languages, we extract response tokens using time-aligned dialogue transcripts, and we analyze their structure with unsupervised methods applied to both the segmental and the prosodic level. Lexical forms are normalized and clustered to reveal each language's inventory of backchannel types and its frequency distribution. For prosody, we extract fundamental frequency contours, duration, and energy from each token and cluster the resulting representations, which lets us ask whether the prosodic categories that emerge, such as flat continuers versus rising or dynamic contours, recur across languages and how they are distributed over lexical forms.

This design allows a controlled comparison across languages precisely because no language-specific categories are imposed. Our analyses address three questions: (i) how large and how skewed the backchannel inventory of each language is, (ii) to what extent form inventories and prosodic categories are shared across languages, and (iii) how much of the variation within a language is carried by lexical choice as opposed to prosodic realization. The results show that all languages examined concentrate most backchannel usage on a small set of frequent forms with a long tail of rarer variants, that a small number of prosodic contour classes recurs across languages, and that languages differ in how they divide functional labor between the lexical and the prosodic channel.

Beyond their descriptive value, these findings matter for speech technology. Dialogue systems that produce backchannels currently rely on hand-picked tokens with fixed realizations, which contributes to their perceived unnaturalness, and listening-oriented systems need to interpret user backchannels whose form varies across languages and speakers [8]. A data-driven map of backchannel structure and variation provides an empirical basis for both generation and understanding, and the proposed methodology can be applied to any language for which conversational audio and transcripts exist.
