A spoken dialogue system must decide not only what to say but also when to say it. Response timing carries communicative meaning: a response that comes too late makes the system appear inattentive, while one that comes too early interrupts the user and disrupts the flow of the interaction [1, 2]. Human interlocutors manage this timing with gaps that are typically only a few hundred milliseconds long [3], and studies of human conversation show that deviations from expected timing are themselves interpreted as meaningful signals, for example as hesitation or disagreement [4]. Despite this, most practical dialogue systems still trigger responses with a fixed silence timeout, an approach that cannot reproduce the flexible timing observed in human dialogue.

A growing body of work addresses this problem by predicting turn endings or response opportunities from acoustic and linguistic cues [5, 6, 7]. These models estimate whether the user has finished a turn, and they substantially improve over silence-based endpointing. However, deciding that the user's turn has ended is only part of the timing problem. In human conversation, the moment a listener chooses to respond is closely tied to listener behavior during the partner's speech, in particular backchannels, the short responses (such as "uh-huh" or "yeah") that listeners produce without taking the turn [8]. Backchannels and full responses occur at similar prosodically marked places, and the choice between producing a backchannel, taking the turn, or remaining silent reflects a common underlying assessment of the dialogue state [9]. This suggests that backchannel prediction and response timing estimation should be modeled jointly rather than in isolation.

In this paper we propose a response timing estimation method for spoken dialogue systems that uses backchannel prediction as an auxiliary signal. Our model continuously processes the user's speech and predicts, at each frame, (i) whether a backchannel opportunity is occurring and (ii) whether the current moment is an appropriate point for the system to begin a response. Both predictions are driven by prosodic features, including fundamental frequency, energy, and their temporal dynamics, together with timing features derived from the ongoing interaction. The intuition is that the prosodic cues that invite listener backchannels, such as phrase-final pitch movements and energy decay, also mark the fine-grained timing structure that a system response should respect. By learning to predict backchannels, the model acquires a representation of these cues that improves the estimation of response timing, even when backchannel labels are unavailable at run time.

We train and evaluate the proposed method on a corpus of human-human dialogue annotated with backchannels and response onsets. Experiments compare the proposed multi-task model against a silence-threshold baseline and a single-task timing model without backchannel supervision. We evaluate both the accuracy of detecting appropriate response points and the timing error of predicted response onsets relative to human responses. The results show that adding backchannel prediction improves response timing estimation, and that prosodic cues account for much of this improvement. We also discuss how the proposed estimator can be integrated into an incremental dialogue system, where its frame-level output can directly drive the decision of when to speak.
